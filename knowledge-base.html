Why does site availability show "N/A" for some months?

Understanding the irradiance threshold and what it means for winter months

The short answer

When you see "N/A" for availability, it means there wasn't enough sunlight during that period to calculate a meaningful availability figure. This typically happens in December and January in the UK when daylight hours are shortest.

Why this happens

Metris only calculates availability when irradiance (sunlight intensity) is above 100 W/mÂ². Below this threshold, solar panels produce negligible power regardless of whether they're working correctly or not. Including these low-light periods would distort your availability figures and make it impossible to distinguish between a system fault and simply not enough sun.

Think of it this way: You wouldn't judge a car's reliability by whether it starts at 3am when you're asleep. Similarly, we don't judge solar system availability when there's barely any sun to capture.

What you should do

Nothing. This is expected behavior and doesn't indicate any problem with your system. When comparing monthly performance, focus on months where availability data is available. For year-over-year comparisons, compare the same months to account for seasonal variation.

Understanding Performance Ratio (PR)

What PR means, how we calculate it, and what's considered "good"

What is Performance Ratio?

Performance Ratio tells you how efficiently your solar system converts available sunlight into electricity, compared to what it would produce under perfect conditions. It's expressed as a percentage.

If your PR is 80%, that means your system is capturing 80% of the energy it theoretically could, given the amount of sunlight hitting the panels.

How we calculate it

The formula is straightforward:

PR = (Actual Energy Output Ã· Expected Energy Output) Ã— 100

Expected output is calculated using irradiance data (how much sun hit your panels) and your system's rated capacity. We source irradiance data from SolarGIS, which provides satellite-based measurements specific to your site location.

Important: Like availability, we only calculate PR when irradiance is above 100 W/mÂ² to ensure meaningful comparisons.

What's a "good" PR?

  • 75-85% - Typical for well-performing commercial rooftop systems in the UK
  • 85%+ - Excellent performance, often seen in newer systems with premium components
  • Below 70% - Worth investigating for potential issues

Note: PR naturally varies with seasons. Summer PR is typically lower than winter because panels lose efficiency as they heat up. Don't compare July PR to January PR directly.

Why PR might look "wrong"

Common reasons for unexpected PR values:

  • Shading: Trees, buildings, or equipment casting shadows on panels
  • Soiling: Dirty panels reduce output
  • System design: The rated capacity in Metris doesn't match what's actually installed
  • Curtailment: If your system is being curtailed, actual output is reduced but expected output isn't adjusted (see our article on curtailment)

What are exclusions and how do they affect my data?

Understanding when and why certain periods are excluded from calculations

What exclusions do

Exclusions remove specific time periods from performance calculations. When a period is excluded, it's as if that time didn't exist for the purposes of calculating PR, availability, and other metrics.

Why exclusions matter

Without exclusions, your performance data would be unfairly penalized for things outside your control. If there's a grid outage and your system can't export power, that's not a fault with your solar installation - it shouldn't drag down your PR.

Types of exclusions

  • Grid outages: When the DNO takes the grid offline for maintenance
  • Force majeure: Extreme weather events, flooding, etc.
  • Contractual curtailment: When you're required to limit output under G100 or similar agreements
  • Planned maintenance: Scheduled work that requires the system to be offline

Investor reports: Exclusions are particularly important for contractual PR calculations. Most PPAs specify which events can be excluded when calculating whether you've met performance guarantees.

How to view exclusions

Adding exclusions

Exclusions can be added by your account manager or, in some cases, directly through the platform. Contact us if you need to add an exclusion for a documented event.

Alerts & Faults

Understanding fault alerts and what action to take

How to interpret alerts and prioritize your response

How alerts work

Metris continuously monitors your portfolio and generates alerts when something needs attention. Each alert includes the affected site, the type of issue, and an estimated revenue impact so you can prioritize effectively.

Alert types

Inverter Offline

The inverter has stopped communicating or reporting production. This could mean the inverter has faulted, lost connection to the monitoring system, or been manually switched off.

Underperformance

The site is producing significantly less than expected given current weather conditions. This is calculated by comparing actual output to expected output from similar sites or historical baselines.

Communication Loss

We can't retrieve data from the monitoring system. This is often a connectivity issue (router, SIM card, internet) rather than an equipment fault.

Revenue impact: The number shown is an estimate based on typical production and current energy prices. Use it to prioritize which alerts to address first.

  4. Track resolution: The alert will clear automatically once the issue is resolved

Why is there missing data for my site?

Common causes of data gaps and how to resolve them

Where data comes from

Metris pulls data from your inverters' monitoring systems via API. Each inverter manufacturer (SolarEdge, Huawei, Solis, etc.) has their own monitoring portal, and we connect to these to retrieve your generation data.

Common causes of missing data

Connectivity issues at site

The most common cause. The inverter is working fine but can't upload data because the internet connection is down, the router has failed, or a SIM card has expired.

Monitoring system outages

Sometimes the manufacturer's portal has issues. When SolarEdge or Huawei have platform problems, it affects data retrieval for all connected sites.

API credential changes

If passwords or API keys are changed in the manufacturer portal, our connection breaks until credentials are updated.

New equipment not yet configured

Newly installed inverters need to be added to your Metris account before data appears.

Good news: Data is rarely lost permanently. Most monitoring systems store data locally on the inverter and will backfill once connectivity is restored.

ðŸ“· Screenshot: Data gap shown on site timeline

How to fix it

  • Check the manufacturer portal: Can you see data there? If yes, the issue is on our end. If no, the issue is at the site.
  • Site connectivity: Ask your O&M provider to check router/modem status and internet connection
  • SIM cards: If the site uses cellular, verify the SIM is active and has credit
  • Contact us: If data is in the manufacturer portal but not in Metris, let us know and we'll investigate

First check the manufacturer portal. If data is there, contact Metris support. If not, arrange a site visit to check connectivity.

How does export curtailment affect my reported numbers?

Understanding G100 limits and their impact on performance metrics

What is export curtailment?

Some sites have grid connection agreements (like G100) that limit how much power can be exported. When your solar system could produce more than this limit allows, the excess is "curtailed" - the system throttles output to stay within the allowed export level.

How it affects your data

Curtailment creates a gap between what your system could produce and what it actually exports. This can make performance metrics look worse than they should:

  • PR looks low: Because actual output is reduced, but expected output (based on irradiance) isn't
  • Generation vs export mismatch: The inverter reports more generation than the meter shows exported

Important: If your site has a G100 agreement, make sure your Metris account manager knows the export limit. This helps us provide more accurate analysis.

How we handle curtailment

For sites with known export limits, we can:

  • Flag when curtailment is likely occurring
  • Calculate "adjusted PR" that accounts for the export limit
  • Show estimated lost revenue from curtailment

Spotting curtailment in your data

Signs that curtailment is happening:

  • Export flatlines at a consistent level during peak sun hours
  • Generation exceeds export (if you have both data sources)
  • PR drops significantly on the sunniest days

If your site has an export limit that isn't reflected in Metris, contact your account manager to have it configured. This will improve the accuracy of your performance reporting.

Why don't my generation and export figures match?

Understanding the difference between what's produced and what's exported

The basics

Generation is the total electricity your solar panels produce. Export is the electricity that flows back to the grid. These are measured at different points and often don't match.

Why they differ

On-site consumption

If the building uses some of the solar electricity directly, that portion is consumed on-site and never reaches the export meter. Generation will be higher than export.

System losses

A small amount of energy is lost in cables, transformers, and other equipment between the inverter (where generation is measured) and the export meter. Typically 1-3%.

Curtailment

If the system is curtailed, generation is throttled to meet export limits. See our article on curtailment for details.

Meter vs inverter accuracy

Inverters and meters have different accuracy standards. Fiscal meters (used for billing) are typically more accurate than inverter readings.

Rule of thumb: Export being 1-5% lower than generation is normal. Larger discrepancies warrant investigation.

ðŸ“· Screenshot: Generation vs export comparison chart

Which number should I use?

  • For billing/revenue: Always use export meter data - this is what you get paid for
  • For system health: Generation data shows true inverter output, useful for diagnosing equipment issues
  • For PR calculations: Depends on your contract - check what's specified in your PPA

âœ“ Action

If export is more than 5% below generation consistently, investigate potential metering issues or excessive system losses.

How meter data flows into Metris

Understanding where your data comes from and how often it updates

Data sources

Metris aggregates data from multiple sources to give you a complete picture:

Inverter monitoring systems

We connect to manufacturer portals (SolarEdge, Huawei, Solis, Enphase, etc.) via API to retrieve generation data. This typically updates every 15 minutes.

Export meters

For sites with half-hourly meters, we pull data from your supplier or metering agent (like Stark). This is the billing-grade data used for revenue calculations.

Weather data

We source irradiance data from SolarGIS, a satellite-based service that provides solar radiation measurements specific to your site's location. This enables accurate expected output calculations.

ðŸ“· Screenshot: Data flow diagram

Update frequency

  • Inverter data: Every 15-60 minutes, depending on manufacturer
  • Meter data: Daily (half-hourly data arrives next day)
  • Weather data: Hourly
  • Alerts: Checked continuously, triggered within minutes of an issue

Note: There's always a small delay between real-time events and data appearing in Metris. If an inverter goes offline at 2pm, you might not see it reflected until 2:15-2:30pm.

Adding new data sources

To connect a new site or data source:

  1. Ensure the inverter monitoring is set up and reporting to the manufacturer portal
  2. Provide your Metris account manager with API credentials or portal access
  3. We configure the connection and verify data is flowing
  4. Historical data is backfilled where available

Data quality checks

Metris automatically flags potential data quality issues:

  • Missing readings
  • Values outside expected ranges
  • Mismatches between generation and export
  • Timestamp inconsistencies
